Bird of the week!

The piping plover (Charadrius melodus)

Quick Facts

  • Piping plovers are a small, endangered shorebird that range from Canada to the Carribean.
  • They are migratory, spending winters in the southern United States and and summers in the northern United States and Canada.
  • There are an estimated 8000 piping plovers remaining.
  • Piping plovers create shallow nests on sandy beaches, leaving them prone to predation and human traffic.
  • These birds have a vocal repetoire of 14 calls! (wow!) Calls are typically used for alarming others and remaining in contact.

Field Work

  • Monitoring piping plover (Charadrius melodus) nesting sites.
    • Call types were classified by recording vocalizations and behaviors. A variety of call types were recorded, each broadly being used in the context of alarms or maintaining contact.
    • Recordings were continuous during the nesting season. Classified calls during field observations were used to monitor rates of each call type.
    • Call rates, of each call type, were plotted before, during, and after aircraft noise to determine if piping plovers were more likely to utter a specific type of call (alarm or contact) during the anthropgoenic noise, or reduce calling rates all together.
    Adult piping plover (Charadrius melodus) photographed at Rogers Beach, New York. Photo credit: Melissa Leone. Terrestrial recording units (SWIFTs) were placed at each nesting site. All recorders were within 20 feet of the nest. Piping plovers are an endangered species. On Long Island, thier nesting sites, as well as potential nesting area, are protected by symbolic fencing.
  • Monitoring of aircraft noise at piping plover nesting sites.
    • Piping plovers that nest on Long Island beaches are subject to constant aircraft noise, typically in the form of commuters flying between Manhattan and the Hamptons, or advertistment planes flying over beachgoers. Flight paths overlap with suitable nesting habitat. Aircraft noise was recorded for each aircraft type seen, leading to three broad classifcations: helicopter, prop plane, and jet plane. Field classifications allowed for later spectrogram analysis, leading to counts of each aircraft type recorded at each nesting site.
